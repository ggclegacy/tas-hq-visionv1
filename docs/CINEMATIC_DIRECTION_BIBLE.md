# TAS HQ Executive Vision Experience

## Cinematic Direction Bible

**Status:** Permanent creative standard  
**Authority:** Highest-priority creative reference for every future act, scene, shot, asset, and build-sequence document  
**Audience:** Blair Vidrine and Bailey Soileau, The Apothecary Shoppe  
**Presented by:** Gent Ascend Collective

---

## 1. Purpose and authority

This Bible defines the permanent creative language of the TAS HQ Executive Vision Experience. It prevents the production from drifting into a website, slideshow, dashboard walkthrough, title-card sequence, or generic technology demo.

Every future build-sequence document must:

1. Read this entire Bible before planning or implementation.
2. Name it as the highest-priority creative reference.
3. Translate its rules into explicit acceptance criteria for the act being built.
4. Preserve the deterministic Presentation Director and established production architecture.
5. Treat existing visuals as replaceable when they fail this standard.
6. Record any intentional exception, its scope, reason, and Neil's explicit approval.

Unless Neil explicitly overrides a named rule, this Bible outranks prior visual implementations, generic framework conventions, creative assumptions, and component defaults. Technical safety, accessibility, asset integrity, and deterministic playback remain non-negotiable alongside this authority.

This document directs future implementation. It does not authorize implementation by itself.

## 2. Product vision

TAS HQ is a directed executive film rendered through real software systems. It is not software with cinematic decoration. It is a carefully paced story that reveals the value of TAS HQ without requiring the audience to discover that value themselves.

It should feel closer to a luxury automotive reveal, an Apple launch film, an advanced aerospace presentation, a premium documentary title sequence, and a private executive keynote built for two people.

The interface is real, but it enters as evidence inside the film. The Presentation Director controls time, narration, captions, shots, environment, interface choreography, transitions, and completion. Optional exploration follows only after the directed story earns it.

### Central proposition

TAS HQ is not another piece of software. It is an operating system for people: a way to preserve, carry, and strengthen the knowledge, service, leadership, and standard The Apothecary Shoppe has already built.

### Human thesis

Technology does not replace culture. It helps people carry culture with consistency as the organization grows.

## 3. Audience and emotional goals

Blair and Bailey are busy executives. The experience requires one deliberate action—**Begin the Presentation**—and almost no effort afterward. It must respect their time, intelligence, company, and achievements.

The emotional arc is:

1. **Recognition:** This understands what we built.
2. **Respect:** This treats our work and people with dignity.
3. **Tension:** Growth makes the standard harder to carry naturally.
4. **Possibility:** Complexity can become coherent.
5. **Proof:** TAS HQ works in daily life.
6. **Trust:** Onyx is governed, transparent, and responsible.
7. **Legacy:** The culture can be preserved and strengthened.

The audience should finish feeling seen, energized, confident, and curious—not lectured, sold to, or exhausted.

Use confidence over hype, precision over spectacle, warmth over sterility, tension without criticism, intelligence without jargon, awe without science-fiction excess, and restraint without emptiness.

## 4. Cinematic philosophy

### Design shots, not screens

Every authored moment is a shot with a subject, point of view, spatial composition, lighting state, movement, sound intention, narrative function, and motivated transition. A new line of copy does not automatically deserve a new shot.

Every shot must answer:

1. What should the audience feel?
2. What new idea or proof does it deliver?
3. Where is the camera and what has visual priority?
4. What occupies foreground, midground, and background?
5. What is already moving before the primary reveal?
6. How does light reveal material and meaning?
7. What does Onyx say, and what should remain unprinted?
8. What changes while the audience reads or listens?
9. What visual or sonic carrier connects to the next shot?
10. Could the shot be removed without losing meaning? If yes, remove it.

### One continuous world

Acts are chapters of one film, not separate pages. Architecture, light, material, atmosphere, sound, camera momentum, or transformation of the previous subject must carry continuity. The next shot should begin forming before the previous shot feels discarded.

### Visual storytelling ratio

Across the experience, target approximately 70% cinematic visual storytelling, 20% interface/product demonstration, and no more than 10% title-led presentation. Narration and visuals carry the story. On-screen copy marks only ideas worth remembering.

### Restraint is not inactivity

A quiet shot may use camera travel, changing reflections, internal illumination, atmospheric flow, focus development, or moving shadow. A still logo on a flat gradient is not restraint; it is unfinished direction.

## 5. Storytelling rules

### Narrative architecture

- **Prologue:** Exclusivity, threshold, and TAS HQ emblem reveal.
- **Act I — The Standard:** Honor trust, knowledge, culture, people, and leadership. Do not introduce software.
- **Act II — The Challenge:** Show growth creating inevitable complexity without blame.
- **Act III — The Vision:** Reveal TAS HQ as an operating system for people and introduce Service, Standard, Collective, and Growth.
- **Act IV — Daily Headquarters:** Prove daily value through directed interface choreography.
- **Act V — Meet Onyx:** Establish a digital coworker with source transparency and responsible escalation.
- **Act VI — The Apothecary Standard:** Show coaching, scenarios, service improvement, and development without childish gamification.
- **Act VII — Knowledge Intelligence:** Show approved knowledge, ownership, effective dates, confidence, and retrieval.
- **Act VIII — Leadership:** Show communication, consistency, recognition, learning insight, and operational visibility without a dashboard wall.
- **Act IX — The Future:** Return to people, culture, legacy, and the TAS HQ emblem.

### Narrative economy

- Each act has one primary narrative job.
- Each sequence changes understanding, emotion, or evidence.
- Combine related ideas visually instead of assigning one sentence to one screen.
- Do not explain what the audience can clearly see.
- Human meaning precedes technical capability; evidence follows promise.
- Do not introduce feature lists before the system matters emotionally.
- Never criticize current operations or imply leadership failure.
- The challenge is a natural result of growth.
- Responsible limits build trust; they are not disclaimers.

Each act should normally contain three to five substantive sequences: pickup, orientation, development, hero turn, and exit carrier. Do not create ten to seventeen text scenes where three visual sequences would work.

## 6. Camera language

The camera is a confident, deliberate, curious executive observer. It reveals intelligence rather than showing off technology.

### Preferred moves

- Controlled dolly or push toward significance.
- Slow lateral travel revealing depth and relationship.
- Purposeful macro-to-wide or wide-to-macro contrast.
- Motivated focal shifts between human, knowledge, and system layers.
- Architectural parallax using bounded 2.5D depth.
- Precision lock when separate elements become coherent.
- Restrained pullback to reveal scale or consequence.
- Purposeful stillness at a true hero moment.

### Rules

- The camera has mass and momentum.
- Movement begins and resolves with credible easing.
- Heavy architecture moves more slowly than light or information.
- Travel must reveal information, change emotional scale, or motivate transition.
- Do not repeat the same push and focal placement across consecutive shots.
- Maintain spatial continuity unless a deliberate cut creates contrast.
- Direct seeking must resolve the same camera state at the same canonical time.

### Forbidden behavior

No random orbiting, constant floating, endless slow zooms, unmotivated rotation, handheld simulation, gratuitous whip pans, unstable logo perspective, or animation-completion-driven playback.

## 7. Shot composition

Composition establishes hierarchy before motion begins.

### Spatial layers

- **Foreground:** controlled occlusion, scale, framing, or transition material.
- **Midground:** primary subject or active relationship.
- **Background:** architecture, depth, distant intelligence, and continuity.

Not every shot needs visible objects in all three layers, but every shot needs believable depth or purposeful graphic flatness. Empty gradients are not environments.

### Framing principles

- Use thirds, asymmetry, negative space, off-axis subjects, close detail, and architectural scale.
- Center logos for ceremonial hero moments when centered authority is intentional.
- Do not mechanically center all typography and subjects.
- Do not mechanically place all copy in the lower-left.
- Composition responds programmatically to viewport and content length.
- Text and subject are composed together, not independently positioned.
- The eye knows what matters within the first beat.
- Protect caption, control, device-safe, and brand zones.

Across a sequence, vary macro detail, hero object, human relationship, wide context, and interface evidence without turning the film into unrelated styles.

## 8. Logo treatment

`gac-logo.png` and `tas-hq-logo.png` are protected brand assets. Never redraw, trace, stretch, squash, destructively crop, indiscriminately recolor, or replace them.

### Logos are physical hero artifacts

Integrate them through confident scale, dimensional separation, controlled parallax, moving rim/specular light, restrained glow, subtle breathing or energy pulse, architectural reflection, foreground occlusion, and motivated entrances and exits.

Masks, light passes, depth, glow, reflection, shadow, and particles tied to light are permitted. Do not spin the flat source image like a coin. Rotating treatments should belong to environmental rings, light, shadow, or constructed depth around the protected artwork unless an approved dimensional asset exists.

### Hero behavior

- Identify a primary emblem within approximately 1.2 seconds of reveal start.
- Resolve logo entry within roughly 2–3 seconds.
- Keep it alive during the hold through light, depth, pulse, or camera behavior.
- Use a commanding scale without colliding with copy or safe areas.
- Exit by camera travel, occlusion, transformation, light loss, or architecture—not a generic fade.
- The TAS HQ emblem may become a gateway, system core, or transition carrier.

No nightclub glow, rapid pulsing, rubbery scaling, constant spinning, particle explosions, or excessive bloom. The logo is powerful because the environment responds to it.

## 9. Environment design

The film occurs inside a persistent executive intelligence environment, not against interchangeable backgrounds.

### World character

Combine premium medical architecture, luxury automotive engineering, advanced aerospace intelligence, executive command infrastructure, and precision-machined technology. It must feel worthy of The Apothecary Shoppe, never like a generic spaceship, game menu, pharmacy ad, or startup landing page.

### Environmental vocabulary

- Obsidian and graphite architecture.
- Recessed apothecary-green intelligence channels.
- Controlled champagne-gold edge detail.
- Selective smoked glass.
- Machined frames, rails, apertures, rings, and chambers.
- Layered shadow volumes and distant silhouettes.
- Warm ivory light where human meaning enters.
- Fine atmosphere visible only in motivated light.
- Information paths behaving like governed flow, not neon decoration.

### Persistent life

At least one subtle environmental behavior should be visible immediately and continue developing through holds: reflection travel, edge light, distant alignment, localized fog, recessed energy flow, shifting shadow, controlled parallax, or a restrained activation pulse.

Avoid pills, bottles, medical crosses, molecule wallpaper, pharmacy shelving, stock clinicians, and other healthcare shorthand unless an approved product demonstration specifically requires real content.

## 10. Materials

Materials must feel physical rather than like universal CSS effects.

- **Obsidian:** structure, depth, and silence.
- **Machined graphite/dark titanium:** primary architecture.
- **Champagne gold/refined brass:** significance and precision.
- **Apothecary green illumination:** trusted intelligence and living knowledge.
- **Warm ivory:** language and human clarity.
- **Smoked glass:** controlled separation and reveal.
- **Satin ceramic:** selective medical calm.

Metal appears through edge light, directional reflection, and contrast—not flat fill. Gold uses clean highlights, a warm metallic body, and restrained bronze shadow; it must never read as mustard, orange, brown-yellow, or matte beige. Glass requires a functional reason. Imperfections are minute and controlled. Do not apply glassmorphism, grain, glow, or gradients everywhere.

## 11. Lighting

Lighting directs attention, meaning, material, and transition.

- **Key light:** defines subject and primary material.
- **Rim light:** separates logos and architecture from darkness.
- **Intelligence light:** green traveling through trusted systems.
- **Significance light:** gold marking leadership, standards, or hero reveals.
- **Human light:** warm ivory for care, people, and legacy.
- **Shadow:** preserves mystery, scale, and restraint.

Do not light the frame evenly. Subjects require separation. Light changes motivate reveals and transitions. Reflections follow implied sources. Bloom is localized. Green is selective illumination, not a flat wash. Gold is material and accent, not wallpaper. Dark scenes must remain legible on ordinary phones and presentation displays.

## 12. Color system

- **Obsidian black:** authority, depth, silence.
- **Graphite:** structure and machinery.
- **Apothecary green:** governed knowledge, connection, and guidance.
- **Champagne gold:** importance, standards, leadership, ceremony.
- **Warm ivory:** primary language and human clarity.

Primary copy is normally warm ivory. Reserve gold for key words, edges, emblem response, or major milestones. Give green tonal range and localized sources. Preserve deep neutral blacks for contrast. Avoid saturated green floods, neon lime, muddy olive, orange gold, bright blue HUDs, and pure-white glare. Verify on desktop, mobile, low brightness, and presentation displays. Never communicate meaning with color alone.

## 13. Typography hierarchy

Typography is part of cinematography, placed in space and timed with the shot rather than pasted onto a background.

### Character

Executive, refined, contemporary, and highly legible. Warm ivory is the default. Weight survives glow, compression, projection, and mobile viewing. Tracking is controlled; excessive spacing does not create luxury. Avoid gaming fonts, decorative science-fiction faces, ultra-thin display type, and generic SaaS styling.

### Hierarchy

1. **Act marker:** quiet orientation, never the hero.
2. **Hero phrase:** one memorable idea, normally one or two lines.
3. **Supporting phrase:** only when narration and visuals cannot carry context.
4. **Interface text:** authentic, crisp, and functional.
5. **Captions:** independently readable narration support.

### Density and reveal

- Prefer hero phrases of 3–8 words.
- A cinematic title moment should generally not exceed 12 words.
- Do not split a simple sentence across delayed beats unless the pause changes meaning.
- Do not print full narration as oversized title copy.
- Avoid paragraph text in cinematic shots.
- If reading requires more than about 3 seconds, justify why it is not narration, interface content, or visual storytelling.
- No act may become a sequence of one-phrase title cards.
- Primary copy should become fully legible within roughly 600–900ms.
- Reveal complete semantic thoughts using light, mask, focus, depth, or camera relationship—not typewriter gimmicks.

## 14. Copywriting principles

Language is direct, human, Apothecary-specific, and confident.

Use short declarative sentences, concrete stakes, respect for what Blair and Bailey built, and language grounded in patients, people, knowledge, answers, service, leadership, standards, and growth.

Avoid consulting filler, empty innovation language, business-transformation clichés, abstract noun stacks, feature dumps, repeated narration/title copy, unsupported claims, criticism disguised as insight, and poetry that slows comprehension.

Every line must pass:

1. Could this appear in a presentation for any company? If yes, make it specific or remove it.
2. Does it sound credible when Onyx says it aloud? If no, rewrite it.
3. Does it add meaning not already delivered visually? If no, remove it.

Tonal examples, not mandatory script:

- “What Blair and Bailey built lives in the people who carry it.”
- “The knowledge behind every confident answer.”
- “The care behind every patient interaction.”
- “A standard this important cannot be left to chance.”

## 15. Motion grammar

Motion has hierarchy, mass, and cause.

- One dominant motion idea per beat.
- Camera, subject, light, and type do not compete simultaneously.
- Heavy architecture moves slowly and resolves firmly.
- Light and information may move faster but remain controlled.
- Typography arrives decisively and holds without fidgeting.
- Logos have inertia and environmental response.
- Motion carries across transitions so the film does not stop and restart.
- Ease curves feel engineered rather than bouncy.
- Every state derives deterministically from canonical presentation time.

Prefer precision locks, masked reveals, bounded parallax, reflection travel, subtle long-decay pulses, motivated occlusion, environment-to-interface transformations, and meaningful stillness.

Forbid springy SaaS motion, bouncing, repeated scale-fades, constant floating, random particle bursts, rapid pulsing, simultaneous movement without hierarchy, animation-completion progression, and random behavior that breaks replay or seeking.

---

## 16. Pacing budgets

Deliberate pacing never leaves enough dead time for the audience to wonder whether playback stalled.

- Environmental pickup: frame one to 250ms.
- Primary subject identification: within approximately 1.2 seconds.
- Text reveal: usually 600–900ms.
- Logo reveal: usually 2–3 seconds.
- Short phrase hold: usually 1.5–2.5 seconds after full legibility.
- Standard transition: usually 500–1,000ms with overlap.
- Hero pause: longer only while sound, light, camera, or material develops.
- Dead visual hold: 0 seconds.

These are guidance, not templates. Narration and emotion determine final timing.

The Prologue target is approximately 35–50 seconds. Every act must declare a runtime and per-sequence budget before implementation. Every extended hold needs a named purpose. A shot is too long when the audience has understood it and then waits without new development; it is too short when the idea cannot be understood once without effort.

## 17. Transition rules

A transition is the relationship between shots, not an effect placed between them.

Preferred carriers include camera movement through an aperture or emblem, foreground occlusion, traveling light, architecture alignment or division, a reflection becoming an interface, governed knowledge flow, precision lock, material change, shape/motion/sound match cuts, and restrained dissolves justified by memory or continuity.

Begin forming the next shot before discarding the current one. Preserve a carrier within the same emotional movement. Use cuts for purposeful contrast, not missing choreography. Avoid routine black resets and generic crossfades. Do not reuse one transition device mechanically. All transition state derives from Presentation Director time and remains seek-safe.

---

## 18. Environmental animation

Allowed systems include bounded architecture alignment, authored light sweeps, low-frequency fog, deterministic particles localized to light, recessed energy travel, shadow/reflection evolution, slow structural rings around protected emblems, camera-linked parallax, and restrained Onyx-triggered activation.

Use authored or seeded behavior where replay matters. No independent high-frequency loops. Render only the active shot and tier-required layers. Pause when playback is paused or hidden. Reduced motion replaces travel with stable lighting, cuts, and short opacity changes. Essential quality retains meaning without decorative atmosphere.

## 19. Audio philosophy

Audio gives the world weight and Onyx authority without imitating a trailer.

### Hierarchy

1. Onyx narration.
2. Essential semantic and transition cues.
3. Architectural ambience and restrained score.
4. Interface and material micro-sound.
5. Silence.

Use low architectural ambience, restrained tonal movement, distant mechanical texture, precise metallic locks, subtle electrical intelligence, controlled activation pulses, and deliberate silence.

The Begin interaction is the audio-unlock threshold. Final core narration is pre-rendered and deterministic. No live ElevenLabs or live AI dependency belongs in the critical path. Captions and visual storytelling preserve meaning without audio. Cues align with canonical time and survive pause, resume, seek, and interruption. Narration remains clear on laptop speakers and presentation systems.

Forbid corporate music, loud trailer impacts, constant bass drones, action risers, random beeps, sentimental stock piano, and any audio masking narration.

## 20. Onyx performance direction

Onyx is narrator, governed intelligence, and digital coworker. The world appears to listen and respond to him.

He is powerful, calm, highly intelligent, engineered, modern, masculine, captivating, precise, slightly futuristic, and professionally restrained.

Use a slightly deliberate executive pace, crisp endings, short intentional pauses, limited meaningful emphasis, warmth for people/patients/culture/legacy, precision for systems/sources/leadership, and calm boundaries during escalation.

He is never a chatbot, audiobook narrator, meditation guide, movie-trailer announcer, radio host, salesman, villain, ordinary assistant, or robotic TTS voice.

Important phrases may trigger restrained light, architecture, knowledge flow, interface emergence, source reveal, or stillness. Do not attach an effect to every sentence.

Onyx must demonstrate source transparency and responsible escalation. When a pharmacist or leadership decision is required, he provides approved information, states the boundary, and routes the question correctly. He never performs fake omniscience.

## 21. Interface reveal rules

The interface is evidence inside the cinematic world. The film must not abruptly become a website walkthrough.

### Entry

- The camera discovers or arrives at the interface.
- Architecture, glass, light, or emblem geometry transforms into its plane.
- It inherits the world’s material, light, depth, and type language.
- The first state is purposeful and immediately understandable.

### Demonstration

- Show one user outcome at a time.
- Direct attention through camera, focus, light, cursor choreography, and content response.
- Use authentic Apothecary-specific content, not placeholders.
- Keep UI readable at presentation distance and on mobile.
- Demonstrate causality: prompt, action, source, response, result.
- Onyx guides without narrating every obvious action.

### Exit

Interface structure becomes a carrier back into architecture or the next system. Do not shrink a webpage away or dissolve into a blank title card.

Forbid dashboard walls, feature tours, card-grid cinematography, purposeless scrolling, tiny UI, wandering cursors, repeated modals, website-like route changes, lorem ipsum, and fake metrics.

## 22. Mobile and desktop composition

Mobile, tablet, desktop, and executive display are independently directed compositions sharing story, time, copy, and meaning. Mobile is not scaled-down desktop.

### Mobile portrait

- Give the hero confident central or upper-frame scale.
- Use vertical depth and controlled foreground framing.
- Protect copy from captions, controls, and safe areas.
- Author narrow line breaks.
- Reduce lateral travel and extreme parallax.
- Maintain detail without maximum brightness.

### Tablet and older laptop

- Compress lateral depth.
- Preserve hero/copy relationships.
- Avoid effects that require huge negative space.
- Treat this as a primary review environment.

### Desktop and executive display

- Use wider asymmetry, architectural scale, controlled negative space, and stronger parallax.
- Keep essential content within presentation-safe bounds.
- Test at realistic viewing distance.

Viewport profiles may alter crop, focal bias, depth, copy region, logo scale, line breaks, layer visibility, and motion intensity. They must not alter story order, canonical time, meaning, or accessibility.

## 23. Performance constraints for Neil's older Mac

Neil develops and reviews on an older Mac. This changes implementation strategy, not creative ambition.

### Hybrid approach

- Semantic DOM/SVG for type, captions, and readable interfaces.
- Deterministic CSS/timeline motion for bounded 2.5D composition.
- Selective WebGL only where it materially improves a signature moment.
- Optimized pre-rendered video/image sequences for hero complexity too expensive or unreliable in real time.
- Stable poster/fallback states and a graceful silent path.
- No live network dependency during playback.

### Performance rules

- Render only the active shot and required overlap.
- Filter by quality tier before render.
- No permanent high-cost canvas when unused.
- No unbounded particles, enormous textures, excessive shadows, or independent high-frequency loops.
- Preload critical near-term assets; load later acts progressively.
- Size and decode media for actual display needs.
- Pause hidden work and recover deliberately.
- Test cold load, replay, exact seek, backward seek, pause/resume, and interruption.
- Preserve deterministic final frames when decoration is removed.
- Prefer quality per rendered pixel over effect count.
- Do not require Neil's machine to render production-grade 3D offline when pre-rendering is safer.

## 24. Quality tiers

All tiers preserve story, copy, timing, logo integrity, captions, controls, and meaning.

### Essential

Stable architecture, primary subject, logo, typography, captions, interface content, minimal transforms, and premium still lighting. No decorative particles, expensive blur, or unnecessary compositing.

### Enhanced

Default for older laptops and capable phones: layered architecture, bounded parallax, moving light, restrained atmosphere, logo rim/specular behavior, moderate reflections and masks, and no persistent expensive GPU system.

### Cinematic

For capable desktop/display hardware: fuller foreground depth, atmospheric finish, higher-quality reflection/shadow/volumetric suggestion, selective WebGL or pre-rendered hero layers, and restrained grain, bloom, depth of field, or motion blur only when justified.

Every act is reviewed at all tiers. Higher tiers add finish, never essential meaning. Essential must feel art-directed, not empty or broken.

## 25. Accessibility and operational integrity

Accessibility is part of premium execution.

- Meaningful text and marks remain semantic; decoration is hidden from assistive technology.
- Captions are synchronized, high-contrast, available, and above cinematic layers.
- Captions do not compete with title copy.
- Controls support keyboard use, visible focus, and adequate touch targets.
- The experience remains complete without sound.
- Reduced motion preserves every idea through stable composition, lighting, cuts, and short fades.
- Color never carries meaning alone.
- Text remains readable over moving backgrounds.
- Pause, resume, restart, skip, seek, and interruption recovery remain reliable.
- Hidden playback performs no narrative work.
- Direct seeking reconstructs the correct frame without replaying prior animations.
- Production routes do not expose developer controls.

## 26. Forbidden design patterns

Unless Neil explicitly approves a documented exception, prohibit:

### Structure

- Text-card slideshows or one sentence per screen.
- Many screens where three visual sequences suffice.
- Page, section, route, or scroll-driven landing-page thinking.
- Feature dumps and dashboard tours.
- A fresh blank background for every idea.

### Composition

- Centering everything by default.
- Placing all copy bottom-left by default.
- Website heading/paragraph stacks.
- Card grids as cinematic scenes.
- Tiny UI.
- Logos treated as ordinary images above copy.

### Visuals

- Flat green or black gradient backgrounds.
- Static logos.
- Muddy yellow-brown gold.
- Full-screen green washes.
- Generic blue science-fiction HUDs.
- Neon cyberpunk.
- Glassmorphism everywhere.
- Random grids, particles, flares, or bloom.
- Generic pharmacy imagery and stock corporate visuals.

### Motion and transitions

- Generic fades as the dominant language.
- Fade in, sit still, fade out.
- Repeated scale-up entrances.
- Typewriter or word-by-word reveals.
- Awkward delayed halves of a phrase.
- Constant floating, spinning, or pulsing.
- Springy SaaS motion.
- Routine black resets.
- Animation-completion progression.
- Scattered component timers.

### Tone and technology

- Consulting filler, startup clichés, movie-trailer narration, gamification, hype, blame, or unsupported claims.
- Futurism that overwhelms care and professionalism.
- Live AI/voice dependency in the critical path.
- Effects that break deterministic playback.
- Desktop layouts merely shrunk to mobile.
- Quality tiers that remove meaning.
- Visual finish that Neil's older Mac cannot review reliably.

1. State the act's narrative job, emotional movement, runtime, and sequence count.
2. Define visual evidence and shot intentions before implementation.
3. Cite relevant Bible sections in its acceptance criteria.
4. Include mobile, tablet, desktop, tiers, reduced motion, audio-off, and older-Mac requirements from the start.
5. Require deterministic timing and exact seek behavior.
6. When full execution is authorized, continue through all prompts without pausing for subjective approval; Neil visually inspects after the phase is complete.
7. Stop only for a true blocker, missing required credential/asset, destructive ambiguity, or a decision that materially changes scope.
8. Run technical validation without claiming that tests equal creative approval.
9. Self-audit against both completion checklists before declaring completion.
10. Report every intentional deviation; silence is not approval.

Future prompts must be more specific than “make it premium,” “add atmosphere,” or “improve the fades.” They must name the shot, composition, material, light, motion, timing, sound, viewport behavior, and narrative result.

The standard is **cinematic, specific, alive, and unforgettable**.
